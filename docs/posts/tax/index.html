Rethinking Taxation in India: Pathways to Equity and Prosperity

Introduction: A Paradigm Shift in Indian Taxation

India stands at a critical juncture where the need for transformative economic reform is more pressing than ever. The current taxation system, heavily reliant on income tax, indirect taxes, and corporate taxes, has served as the backbone of public revenue. However, it also carries inherent inefficiencies, complexities, and inequities. With a population exceeding 1.4 billion and significant income disparity, only a small fraction of citizens—approximately 6% of the population—contribute to income tax revenues. This narrow taxpayer base, coupled with high levels of informal employment and tax evasion, necessitates rethinking how the government generates and redistributes wealth.

The proposal to abolish income taxes at all levels and implement an alternative system where citizens earn annual payments represents a bold, unprecedented shift in fiscal policy. This paper explores the feasibility of such a system in the Indian context, addressing the challenges, potential benefits, and socio-economic implications. By examining alternative revenue sources, such as an expanded Goods and Services Tax (GST), resource-based revenues, and wealth taxes, this model seeks to ensure fiscal sustainability while promoting equity and economic growth.

The Current Indian Taxation Landscape

Income Tax System: Scope and Challenges

India’s income tax system is progressive, with rates ranging from 5% to 30% based on income slabs. While it aims to ensure equity by taxing higher-income groups at steeper rates, the system faces several challenges:

  1. Low Compliance and Narrow Base: Despite its progressive structure, less than 6% of Indians file income tax returns. This is partly due to the large informal economy, where incomes are either not recorded or fall below taxable thresholds.
  2. Administrative Complexity: High compliance costs, frequent policy changes, and complex procedures deter voluntary compliance.
  3. Tax Evasion and Black Money: The prevalence of unaccounted wealth and underreported incomes undermines the system’s effectiveness.

Indirect Taxes: The GST Experience

The introduction of the Goods and Services Tax (GST) in 2017 marked a historic reform in India’s indirect taxation system. GST subsumed multiple indirect taxes, creating a unified tax structure. However, challenges persist:

  1. Compliance Issues: The transition to GST was marred by technical glitches, and compliance remains burdensome for small businesses.
  2. Regressivity: Indirect taxes like GST tend to disproportionately affect lower-income groups, as they spend a higher proportion of their income on taxable goods.
  3. Revenue Shortfalls: States frequently report revenue deficits due to lower-than-expected GST collections.

Corporate Taxation and Its Limitations

Corporate taxes contribute a significant portion of India’s revenue. Recent reductions in corporate tax rates aimed at boosting investment and economic growth have, however, led to debates about their impact on public revenue and income inequality.

Challenges of the Current System

Inequity and Inefficiency

India’s tax system, while designed to be progressive, often fails to achieve equity due to evasion and reliance on regressive indirect taxes. The burden of indirect taxes disproportionately impacts the poor, exacerbating inequality.

Low Trust in Tax Administration

Perceptions of corruption, inefficiency, and harassment in tax administration undermine public trust and voluntary compliance.

Informal Economy

India’s informal sector accounts for nearly 80% of employment and a significant portion of GDP. This sector remains largely untaxed, contributing to revenue leakages and widening fiscal deficits.

The Case for Reform: Why Abolish Income Taxes?

Streamlining Revenue Collection

Abolishing income taxes could simplify the taxation framework, reducing administrative costs and increasing transparency. By shifting to alternative revenue models, the government can create a system that is easier to enforce and harder to evade.

Inclusivity

A system where all citizens, irrespective of income levels, receive annual payments would promote inclusivity and equity. It would provide financial security to marginalized groups and bridge the rural-urban divide.

Encouraging Formalization

By removing the disincentive of income taxation, businesses and individuals in the informal sector may be encouraged to formalize their operations, thereby contributing to other forms of taxation, such as GST.

Lessons from Global Models

The Alaska Permanent Fund

The Alaska Permanent Fund offers annual dividends to residents from oil revenues. This model demonstrates the feasibility of direct payments funded by natural resource wealth, albeit in a smaller, resource-rich economy.

Gulf Nations

Many Gulf countries rely on indirect taxes, such as VAT, instead of income taxes. Their experience highlights the potential of consumption taxes in resource-dependent economies.

Nordic Countries

While Nordic countries maintain high income taxes, their emphasis on wealth taxes and robust social welfare systems provides insights into designing equitable redistribution mechanisms.

Revenue Sources for the New System

Goods and Services Tax (GST)

Expanding the GST base and improving compliance can significantly increase revenue. For example, reducing exemptions and bringing sectors like real estate and petroleum under GST could generate additional funds.

Natural Resource Revenues

India is rich in natural resources, including coal, minerals, and renewable energy potential. Tapping into these resources through royalties, leases, and environmental taxes can provide a sustainable revenue stream.

Wealth and Property Taxes

Reintroducing or reforming wealth taxes and ensuring proper valuation and collection of property taxes can help address income inequality while generating significant revenue.

Digital Economy Taxation

India’s booming digital economy presents opportunities for innovative taxation methods, such as levies on digital transactions, e-commerce, and data monetization.

Socio-Economic Implications

Poverty Alleviation

Direct payments can provide a safety net for low-income households, reducing poverty and improving access to basic services like education and healthcare.

Consumption and Economic Growth

Increased disposable income through annual payments can boost consumption, driving demand and stimulating economic growth.

Inflationary Risks

While increased consumption can spur growth, it may also lead to inflation. The system must include safeguards to manage inflationary pressures.

Implementation Challenges

Political Resistance

Abolishing income taxes and introducing new revenue streams will face resistance from vested interests and political opposition.

Administrative Feasibility

Implementing a nationwide payment system and ensuring compliance with new taxes will require significant investments in technology and capacity building.

Equity Concerns

The shift to indirect taxes may disproportionately impact lower-income groups. Policymakers must design measures to mitigate regressive effects.

Conclusion

The abolition of income taxes and the introduction of an annual payment system in India represent a bold vision for equitable and inclusive economic reform. By leveraging alternative revenue sources and adopting a people-centric approach, India can address longstanding inefficiencies and inequities in its tax system. However, successful implementation will require careful planning, robust technology infrastructure, and sustained political will.
